===============================================================================
 TILLAGE FIELD ASSESSMENT — WHAT THIS APP IS AND HOW IT WORKS
 Conservation Agriculture, Morocco
 Written 17 July 2026
===============================================================================

This is a plain-language summary for people who will not read the code:
collaborators, supervisors, an ethics board, or you in six months. It covers
what the app does, what it needs, what it collects, how to get the data out,
how everything is backed up, and one open scientific question that has NOT
been settled yet.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. WHAT THE APP DOES
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

It is a web page you send to volunteers by email. There is nothing to install
and no account to create. A volunteer opens the link, types a name, reads a
one-page explanation, and is then shown fields one at a time.

For each field, they see ONE picture made of 8 smaller satellite pictures of
the SAME field, taken every two weeks from 1 September through 8 December.
The 8 pictures are labelled A to H — A, B, C, D across the top row, and
E, F, G, H across the bottom. The field being judged is outlined in bright
magenta, with a dot marking its centre.

They answer one question: was this field tilled (was the soil turned over)?
They can answer "Tilled", "No-till", or "Can't tell".

If — and only if — they answer "Tilled", a second question appears asking in
WHICH of the 8 pictures they first see the tillage. The eight date buttons are
laid out in two rows of four so they sit in the same arrangement as the eight
pictures. There is deliberately no "Can't tell" on this second question: a
person who has just said the field was tilled should be able to point at where
they saw it.

They press Next and get another field. A session is 50 fields, roughly 10–15
minutes. They can stop at any time — everything already answered is kept.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. WHAT IT NEEDS AS INPUT
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Three things, all of which already exist:

  a) THE PICTURES. 500 montages, one per field, for Settat province, season
     2025. They live in the folder "images/Settat/2025/" and are named after
     the field's GPS position, e.g. "32.45172_-7.27861_2025_Settat.jpg".
     They were built from Sentinel-2 satellite imagery in Google Earth Engine.

  b) THE FIELD LIST. A spreadsheet ("pairs_metadata.csv") with one row per
     field: its ID, province, year, picture filename, and GPS coordinates.
     The 500 fields were picked at random from a map of Settat field
     boundaries, deliberately excluding the very smallest and very largest
     fields so the sample is representative.

  c) A NAME. That is the only thing we ask a volunteer to type. No email, no
     password, no account.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. WHAT DATA IT COLLECTS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For every single answer, one row is stored containing:

  - the name the volunteer typed
  - which field they were looking at
  - their answer ("Tilled" / "No-till" / "Can't tell")
  - if tilled: which picture (A–H) they first saw it in
  - the date and time of the answer
  - their IP address
  - technical details of their device: browser, operating system, language,
    timezone, screen and window size, processor cores, memory, touchscreen
    or not

The last two items are personal data under GDPR. This is disclosed to the
volunteer in plain language on the first page, BEFORE they type anything. It
is collected for research quality — it lets us spot, for example, one person
answering under three different names, or answers submitted implausibly fast.
It is stored in the EU and is not published.

Note on location: we do NOT collect precise GPS from the volunteer. A browser
cannot hand that over silently — it always raises a permission pop-up — so we
deliberately do not ask. Approximate location can be worked out later from the
IP address if the analysis needs it.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. HOW TO GET THE DATA OUT
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The answers sit in a Supabase database (a hosted PostgreSQL database, free
tier, servers in the EU).

  THE LIVE ANSWERS — from the Supabase dashboard:
      1. SQL Editor -> New query
      2. Paste the whole of the file "supabase/export_query.sql"
      3. Run, then click "Download CSV"
      4. Save it as "responses_export.csv" in the project folder

    Use that query, NOT the "Export -> CSV" button on the responses table.
    The button dumps the table exactly as stored, which keeps all the answers
    inside a single JSON blob and has no field identifier column — the
    analysis script cannot read it and stops with an error. The query above
    unpacks it into one column per question, which is what the analysis
    expects. (The script now detects the wrong shape and tells you so, rather
    than failing cryptically.)

  THE LOCAL TEST ANSWERS — from your machine:
      python export_responses.py
    This reads the local test database (data/study.db) ONLY. It cannot reach
    Supabase: the app's public key is deliberately not allowed to read the
    responses table, which is what stops a stranger downloading everyone's
    answers. For live data, use the SQL query above.

  FOR ANALYSIS (either source — both produce identical columns):
      Rscript r/analyze_responses.R
    This reports how many fields have been covered, the spread of answers,
    when in the season people say tillage first appears, agreement between
    labelers who saw the same field (Cohen's kappa), and some quality checks
    over IP addresses, timezones and answer times.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. BACKUP STRATEGY
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

There are four independent copies, so no single failure loses the work:

  1. THE CODE AND PICTURES are in a public GitHub repository
     (github.com/Floriandbl/notill-validation). Every change is a permanent,
     reversible commit. The website is served directly from that repository
     by GitHub Pages — so the thing volunteers use and the thing that is
     backed up are literally the same files.

  2. THE ANSWERS are in Supabase, which takes its own daily backups.

  3. THE OLD ANSWERS from the earlier placeholder round (63 answers from 3
     people, collected against test images) are preserved in a side table
     called "responses_placeholder_backup" rather than deleted.

  4. YOUR LOCAL COPY of the whole project, in Dropbox, which is itself
     versioned.

  Exports (step 4 above) are cheap — running the export regularly and keeping
  the dated CSVs is a sensible fifth layer, and costs nothing.

  ONE RISK WORTH KNOWING: the Supabase free tier pauses a project after 7 days
  of no activity, and a paused project makes the app go completely dead — this
  has already happened once. Before circulating the link widely, either plan to
  keep it active, or accept that it may need waking up from the dashboard.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. MAIN FEATURES
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  - No install, no account. One link, works on a phone or a computer.
  - Fair distribution of work: each volunteer is handed a batch of 50 fields,
    and the database hands out different fields to different people at the
    same time without collisions.
  - Nobody sees the same field twice.
  - Answers save one at a time, as you go. Closing the tab loses nothing.
  - Follow-up question that only appears when it is relevant.
  - Tap-to-enlarge viewer. On a phone the montage is only about 330 pixels
    wide, which makes each of the 8 pictures roughly 80 pixels — too small to
    judge. Tapping opens it about three times larger, and it can be pinched
    and panned.
  - Designed for phones: no sideways scrolling, finger-sized buttons, and the
    Next button stays within reach at the bottom of the screen.
  - A live progress panel on the front page: how many contributors, how many
    fields done, how many left.
  - Everything that decides how the study behaves — the questions, the
    answer options, the intro text, the batch size — lives in ONE file
    ("static/config.js") that can be edited without touching any programming
    logic.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. OPEN QUESTION — PLEASE READ BEFORE SENDING THE LINK OUT
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The app is finished and works. Whether the PICTURES can answer the question is
not yet established, and this is the real risk to the project.

We measured all 500 montages. Two results stand out:

  - Every field looks broadly the same across the season. All 500 start out
    the same colour in September and drift the same way by December. The
    change you see is mostly the dry summer soil greening up after the autumn
    rains — the weather, not the plough.

  - Of 500 fields, only one showed a clear, sudden change of the kind tillage
    should produce. When we looked at that one closely, the change covered the
    whole picture, not just the field — so it was the regional December
    green-up too, not tillage.

In short: the satellite pictures are dominated by the season. Tillage, if it
is in there at all, is a much smaller effect, and the Sentinel-2 satellite's
10-metre resolution is coarse relative to these fields (typically 0.75
hectares, about 9 pixels across).

Because of this, the two example images on the "How to tell them apart" screen
are marked in red as placeholders. They were chosen automatically as the most
and least changeable fields — they are NOT confirmed cases of tillage and
no-till, and a Remote Sensing specialist needs to replace them. The "How to
read the images" section is likewise a deliberate placeholder rather than a
guess.

RECOMMENDED NEXT STEP: take about 20 fields where the technicians already know
the ground truth, and have two people who understand the agronomy label them
using this app. If experts cannot beat a coin flip on this imagery, volunteers
will not either, and we would be spending our volunteers' goodwill — which we
only get to spend once — on noise. If it fails, the fallback is Planet
Explorer & Research: 3-metre imagery, near-daily, free for university
researchers, roughly three weeks to be approved, and our 500 fields over 8
dates fit comfortably inside the free monthly quota.
